# Users & Usage

## Personas

### Dana — weekend day-hiker (primary)
- Background: works full-time, hikes 2-3 weekends a month within ~90 minutes of home.
- Current workaround: checks the park website, an AllTrails review feed, and a local Facebook group the night before.
- Tech comfort: high — lives on her phone, comfortable posting photos.

### Marcus — trail runner, frequent reporter
- Background: runs the same loops 4-5x/week, sees conditions change constantly.
- Current workaround: mentions conditions in a running club group chat that scrolls away.
- Tech comfort: high; willing to log a quick report if it takes under a minute.

### Priya — volunteer trail moderator (editorial)
- Background: member of the local trail association; wants the trail list accurate.
- Current workaround: emails corrections to the webmaster.
- Tech comfort: medium; comfortable in an admin UI, not in code.

## Jobs-to-be-done
- When Dana is planning a Saturday hike on Friday night, she wants the most recent real condition of a specific trail, so she can pick a trail that's actually worth the drive.
- When Marcus finishes a run and saw something notable (downed tree, ice), he wants to log it in under a minute, so others are warned and he feels he contributed.
- When Priya notices a duplicate or mislabeled trail, she wants to fix the canonical trail record, so reports attach to the right place.

## Primary user journey
1. Discovery: Dana hears about Trailhead in the Facebook group; opens the web app, no signup needed to read.
2. First use: searches "Eagle Ridge," sees the trail page with the 3 latest reports and a freshness badge ("last report 3h ago").
3. Aha moment: a report says "creek knee-deep, bring poles" — she changes plans and avoids a bad trip.
4. Ongoing use: she signs up so she can post her own report after that weekend's hike; later she becomes a reporter Marcus-style.

## Core loop
- The repeated action: **check a trail's recent reports → hike → post a report** (condition rating + short note + optional photo).
- What pulls users back: trips are recurring, conditions are always changing, and a freshness badge makes stale trails visibly "unknown" — which nudges regulars to be the one who refreshes it.
